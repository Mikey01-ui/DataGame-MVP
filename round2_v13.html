REAL-WORLD TRANSLATION
You ruled on four data ownership conflicts — using stewardship records, lineage pipelines, and classification flags to determine who is truly accountable for each dataset.

In the real world this is Data Governance: ownership is not determined by who generates data, who stores it, or whose name appears in the filename. It is determined by the Data Steward — the accountable person recorded in the governance registry. But a registry entry is only as good as its verification: one of your disputes had a steward field that named the wrong department because it was auto-filled and never verified. A trustworthy field beats a convenient one. The metadata is the ground truth — but only the part of it you can actually trust.
WHAT YOU LEARNED
1. Origin is not ownership. Where data is created (the OMS) does not decide who governs it.
2. Usage is not ownership. The team that reads the reports is not automatically the steward.
3. A pipe is not an owner. Data flowing through a system (an integration layer) does not transfer stewardship.
4. An unverified field can't settle anything. A steward entry only counts when it is verified — check before you trust it.
5. Regulation overrides everything. GDPR-regulated personal data falls to the DPO regardless of who built the platform.
